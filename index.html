The Regimes Explained

The Indian tax system offers two paths. The Old Regime rewards savings and investments with deductions, while the New Regime (the default choice) offers lower tax rates with fewer deductions. This section helps you understand the core differences beyond the numbers.

Old Tax Regime

This regime is built on the principle of encouraging savings. It allows you to reduce your taxable income by claiming over 70 deductions for various investments and expenses.

  • ✔Full access to deductions like 80C, 80D, HRA, and home loan interest.
  • ✔Ideal for those with high investments and expenses like rent or home loans.
  • ✖Higher tax slab rates compared to the new regime.

New Tax Regime

This regime prioritizes simplicity and lower tax rates. It is the default option and is designed for those who prefer a straightforward tax filing experience.

  • ✔Lower tax rates and more slabs for gradual tax increase.
  • ✔Enhanced standard deduction (₹75,000) and a powerful tax rebate making income up to ₹12 Lakh effectively tax-free.
  • ✖Forgoes most major deductions, including 80C, 80D, and HRA.

Deductions Explorer (Old Regime)

This is your toolkit for tax saving under the Old Regime. Each deduction you claim reduces your taxable income, directly lowering your tax outgo. Explore the most powerful options available in Chapter VI-A of the Income Tax Act.

Section 80C: The Investment Powerhouse (up to ₹1.5 Lakh) +

This is a basket of popular options including PPF, ELSS Mutual Funds, Life Insurance Premiums, Home Loan Principal, and Children's Tuition Fees. A strategic mix can help achieve both tax savings and long-term financial goals.

Section 80D: The Health Guardian +

Allows deduction for health insurance premiums. You can claim up to ₹25,000 for self/family, plus an additional ₹25,000 (or ₹50,000 if they are senior citizens) for your parents' policy. This makes the maximum possible deduction ₹1,00,000.

NPS: The Retirement Booster (up to ₹50,000 extra) +

Section 80CCD(1B) provides an exclusive deduction of ₹50,000 for contributions to the National Pension System. This is *over and above* the ₹1.5 Lakh limit of Section 80C, enabling a total deduction of ₹2 Lakh.

Section 24(b): The Homeowner's Advantage +

Allows deduction for interest paid on a home loan for a self-occupied property, up to a maximum of ₹2,00,000 per year. This is one of the single largest deductions available to taxpayers.
